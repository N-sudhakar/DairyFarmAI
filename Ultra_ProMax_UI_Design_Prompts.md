### Prompt 1.1 — Master Visual Identity and Design Language

You are the Creative Director of a world-class product design studio that has shipped
interfaces for Apple, Stripe, Linear, Vercel, and Notion. You have been commissioned
to design the complete visual identity and design language for the AI Dairy Farm
Management System — a platform that must simultaneously feel like the most advanced
agricultural intelligence system in Asia and the most approachable tool for a farmer
who cares deeply about his cattle.

Define the complete master design language from the ground up with extreme precision
and creative ambition.

The aesthetic direction is what you would get if you crossed the editorial luxury of a
Monocle magazine spread, the precision of a Bloomberg Terminal redesigned by Jony Ive,
and the organic warmth of terracotta pottery from Tamil Nadu. The result is a UI that
is dark, warm, structured, intelligent, and alive. Think deep earth tones with sharp
gold accents and crisp white data. Think the dashboard of a luxury tractor designed
in 2040.

The primary background is not black and not white. It is a very deep warm charcoal with
the slightest hint of green, like the color of the sky just before sunrise over a paddy
field. This color is the foundation of everything. It is applied to the main application
shell, the navigation, and all primary panels.

The surface color sits one step lighter than the background and is used for cards,
panels, and modal backgrounds. It has a subtle warm undertone like weathered teak wood.
Shadows on these surfaces are not black. They are warm dark amber at very low opacity,
giving depth without coldness.

The accent color is a carefully chosen saffron-gold that references the turmeric and
mustard fields of India while feeling completely modern and tech-forward. This color
appears on primary action buttons, active states, chart highlights, and critical data
callouts. It is used sparingly and never diluted. When it appears it commands attention.

The secondary accent is a deep forest green — not the bright green of a vegetable but
the deep dark green of a well-tended mango grove at noon. This color is used for
positive states, growth indicators, healthy cattle markers, and success confirmations.

The alert color for critical warnings is a warm deep red like dried chilli pepper, not
the harsh red of a traffic light. It is used only for genuine alerts that require
immediate action.

The data color palette for charts is a five-color sequence that is warm, distinctive,
and beautiful when all five appear together on a dark background. The colors are
saffron-gold, forest-green, terracotta-orange, sky-at-dusk-blue, and cream-white.
These colors are never washed out. They are full saturation on the dark background.

Typography uses two typefaces exclusively. The display typeface for all headings,
metrics, hero numbers, and callouts is a high-end geometric serif with strong
personality — something in the lineage of Canela, Tiempos, or Freight Display. This
choice signals that data has weight and meaning. It makes a chart title feel like
a declaration. The body typeface for all body copy, labels, table data, and UI text
is a precision grotesque with excellent legibility at small sizes and perfect
numerical tabular figures so all numbers in tables align perfectly.

The spacing system uses an eight-pixel base unit exclusively. All padding, margins,
gaps, and sizes are multiples of eight. This creates perfect visual rhythm across
every screen without exception.

Elevation is communicated through warmth of shadow and blur, not through brightness.
Higher elevation elements have warmer, softer shadows. They do not become lighter.
This maintains the dark-first design language throughout.

Border radius follows a deliberate system. Data-dense components like table cells and
input fields use a two-pixel radius — almost sharp because data should feel precise.
Medium components like cards and panels use an eight to twelve pixel radius. Large
components like modals and sheets use a sixteen to twenty-four pixel radius. The
difference between a chart card and its data label is coded in the border radius.

Output a complete master design language document covering every aspect of the visual
identity including the full color system with exact hex values, the typography pairing
with specific usage rules for every text level, the spacing scale, the shadow and
elevation system, the border radius system, and three conceptual mood board descriptions
that capture the intended aesthetic.

### Prompt 1.2 — Iconography, Illustration, and Data Visualization Style

You are a visual design specialist defining the iconography system, illustration
language, and data visualization aesthetic for the platform.

The icon system uses a single cohesive style throughout the platform. The style is
a precision line icon with a consistent stroke width of one point five pixels, rounded
line caps, and a combination of simple paths with occasional filled accent elements
in the saffron-gold color. Icons are never purely decorative. Every icon communicates
a specific meaning and is paired with a text label except in navigation contexts where
the pairing is well established. The icon family covers six hundred icons minimum
including all standard UI icons, all cattle and farm-specific icons, all agricultural
concepts, all financial concepts, and all AI and data science concepts. Farm-specific
icons must be custom drawn and must feel precise and respectful — a milking cow icon
should look like an actual Holstein not a cartoon emoji.

The illustration system is used only in three contexts — empty states when a list or
dashboard section has no data yet, onboarding screens guiding new users through their
first experiences, and error pages for exceptional situations. The illustration style
is an isometric flat illustration using the platform color palette. Scenes depict Indian
farm landscapes, cattle in beautiful natural settings, data flowing through elegant
abstract networks, and farmers using technology confidently. Every illustration must
feel aspirational and respectful of the farming profession. Never use clip art style.
Never use generic tech startup illustration tropes with floating laptops and thumbs up.

The data visualization style for the platform follows a set of principles that make
every chart feel like it was designed by an information design master. Charts never
use default library styles. Every chart uses the custom color palette. Grid lines are
barely visible — one pixel at four percent opacity on the dark background. Axes are
clean with only as many labels as needed for comprehension. Every chart has a
purposeful title that states the insight not just the metric. For example instead of
Monthly Milk Production the title reads Milk Output Trend — Last 12 Months. Chart
tooltips are beautifully designed — they appear as floating cards with a warm shadow,
showing the exact value, the date, and a brief comparison to the previous period.

Describe the specific chart types used for each data category in the platform. Time
series data for milk production uses a smooth area chart with a gradient fill that
fades from the saffron-gold accent to transparent. Proportional data for cost
breakdown uses a horizontal bar chart with rounded bar ends, never a pie chart.
Performance comparison across cattle uses a tile heat map where each tile represents
one animal colored on a spectrum from deep green for excellent to warm red for concern.
Financial trend data uses a combination bar and line chart with the bars showing
monthly totals and the line showing the running average. Geographic data for farm
location shows on a beautifully styled dark map.

Output a complete visual asset system specification covering the icon style guide with
stroke weights and usage rules, the illustration style guide with scene types and
never-do examples, and the data visualization style guide with chart type assignments
for every data category in the platform.

### Prompt 1.3 — Component Library Ultra Design Specification

You are a senior design systems engineer defining every UI component for the platform
at the highest possible level of craft and detail.

Buttons exist in four variants that feel like they were carved from different materials.
The primary button uses the saffron-gold accent color as its background with deep
charcoal text. It has no border. Its hover state reveals a subtle inner glow that
pulses once and settles. Its pressed state compresses the button height by two pixels
giving a physical click sensation through visual feedback. Its loading state replaces
the label with three dots that animate sequentially not simultaneously. Its disabled
state does not reduce opacity in the standard way — instead the text becomes the
background color as if the text is trying to disappear into the button surface.

The secondary button is a transparent button with the saffron-gold text and a
one-pixel saffron-gold border. On hover the border becomes two pixels and the text
brightens. The interaction feels like activating a circuit.

The ghost button has no background, no border, and uses the warm cream text color.
It is invisible until hovered when a very subtle warm surface appears beneath it.
Used for tertiary actions that should exist but not compete.

The destructive button is deep red with white text. Its hover state makes the red
slightly more saturated — it gets angrier on hover. This communicates that clicking
this button has weight.

Form inputs are not rectangles. They have no visible border in the default state.
Instead they have a warm surface color that is slightly lighter than the card surface
they sit on, giving a material inset quality. The label floats above the field by
default and shrinks to a small size when the field gains focus or has content. The
focus state reveals a single line at the bottom of the input in the saffron-gold
color that sweeps in from left to right in 200 milliseconds. The error state shows
the line in the warm red color. The success state shows it in the forest green color.
The validation message appears below the field in a small italic font, not bold.

Cards are the primary content container. They use the surface color with a very subtle
warm inner border at four percent opacity. On hover cards that are interactive lift
slightly — a two pixel upward translation over 150 milliseconds with a slightly warmer
shadow. Cards that are selected or active get a one-pixel saffron-gold border on all
four sides. Cards that contain alerts get a left border accent — three pixels wide in
the relevant alert color.

Data tables are the most information-dense component. Table headers use the display
typeface at a small size with wide letter spacing — it makes headers feel like column
titles in a financial newspaper. Table rows alternate between the background color and
the surface color at very low contrast — just enough to help the eye track horizontally.
The selected row state fills with a warm saffron tint at eight percent opacity. Sort
indicators are small arrows that only appear on hover of the column header. The
hovered row state shows a very subtle highlight that is slightly warmer than the row
background. Numerical columns in tables always right-align. Text columns left-align.
Status columns center-align with a colored badge.

Badges and tags use the full color palette. They have a four-pixel border radius.
They are eight pixels tall in data tables and twelve pixels tall in detail views.
They never have a border — their background color communicates the status. Text
inside badges uses the body typeface at the smallest size in the scale.

Navigation uses a left sidebar that is sixty-four pixels wide in its collapsed state
showing only icons and two hundred and forty pixels wide in its expanded state showing
icons with labels. The transition between states takes 200 milliseconds with an ease
curve that accelerates then decelerates gently. The active navigation item has a
full-width saffron-gold left border and the icon and text use the saffron-gold color.
Inactive items use the warm cream color at sixty percent opacity and become full
opacity on hover.

Output a complete component library specification covering every component variant,
every interactive state, every animation behavior, every color assignment, and every
typographic treatment for buttons, inputs, cards, tables, badges, navigation,
modals, tooltips, dropdowns, toggles, sliders, progress indicators, and loading states.

### Prompt 2.1 — Login Page as a World-Class First Impression

You are the lead product designer describing the Login Page as if presenting it to
the board of a luxury technology company. This page is the first thing every user sees.
It must create an immediate emotional response of confidence, curiosity, and desire
to use the platform.

The page uses the full dark design language. The background is the deep warm charcoal.
Behind the form on the right side there is a very subtle animated mesh gradient — three
circular gradients in saffron-gold, forest-green, and terracotta-orange at three percent
opacity each, drifting slowly across the background in a breathing motion over forty
seconds. This is the only movement on the page before the user interacts. It makes the
page feel alive without being distracting.

The left half of the page is a full-height visual composition. The top third shows a
stunning high-resolution aerial photograph of an Indian dairy farm at golden hour — the
light is warm and directional, cattle are visible as small figures in lush green fields,
the sky is a deep amber to violet gradient. Over this photograph is a dark gradient
overlay that fades from fully opaque at the bottom to thirty percent opacity at the top
so the image is visible but controlled. The platform logo sits centered in the upper
area of the left panel in pure white. Below the logo is the tagline in the display
typeface at a large size, set in a creamy white. The tagline uses a two-line layout with
the first line smaller and the second line larger, creating a typographic rhythm.

Three feature cards float in the lower third of the left panel. These are not bullet
points. Each card is a small dark glass-morphism panel — a frosted glass effect using
backdrop blur on the dark overlay. Each card has an icon in the saffron-gold color, a
one-line feature name in the display typeface, and a two-line benefit description in
the small body typeface. The three cards are arranged in a slight diagonal composition
where the middle card is elevated above the other two — not a straight horizontal row.

The right half is the form area. The form is centered both horizontally and vertically
in the right half. It has no outer card border. The form elements float on the surface
with generous vertical spacing between them. The top of the form area shows a small
version of the platform mark — the icon without the wordmark — in a warm cream color.

The greeting text Welcome Back is set in the display typeface at a large size. Below it

The email and password fields use the inset input design from the component specification.
Between the fields is a subtle dividing line at four percent opacity. The Remember Me
checkbox uses a custom checkbox design — a square with rounded corners that fills with
the saffron-gold color and shows a custom checkmark when selected.

The Sign In button is the most visually prominent element on the right side. It is full
width, tall at fifty-six pixels, and uses the primary button design. At the moment the
user successfully enters valid credentials and the button is clicked, the button fills
from left to right with a sweeping animation before transitioning to the loading state.
After authentication the entire right panel dissolves upward while the left panel photo
expands to fill the full screen for a split second before the dashboard loads.

The social login buttons for Google and Microsoft sit below a hairline divider with an
OR label centered on it. These buttons use the ghost button style with the respective
brand logos.

The Register Now link at the bottom uses the saffron-gold color and has an underline
that animates in from left to right on hover only.

Output a complete Login Page ultra design specification covering every visual element,

## 3. REGISTRATION PAGE ULTRA DESIGN PROMPTS

---

### Prompt 3.1 — Registration as an Immersive Onboarding Journey

You are a senior experience designer describing the Registration Page as a carefully
choreographed journey that makes a new farmer feel like they are being personally
welcomed into something exceptional.

The registration page abandons the conventional form layout entirely. Instead it uses
a full-screen immersive layout where the step indicator is not a horizontal bar at the
top but a vertical timeline on the left side of the page — a thin vertical line in the
saffron-gold color with three circular nodes on it. The active node is filled and
slightly larger. Completed nodes are filled with a checkmark. The future node is
outlined only. The step labels sit to the right of each node. This vertical timeline
sits fixed on the left side throughout all three steps.

The form area on the right slides horizontally. When moving from step one to step two
the current step's fields slide off to the left and the next step's fields slide in
from the right. The transition takes three hundred milliseconds with a smooth ease
curve. The animation makes the form feel like chapters of a story being turned.

Behind the entire page is a full-screen background composition. It is not a photograph.
It is a large-scale data visualization of a fictional farm — a beautiful abstract
network diagram showing cattle as glowing nodes connected by thin lines representing
their relationships and data flows. The nodes pulse gently at different rates. This
visualization is rendered in the platform color palette at very low opacity — it is
atmospheric, not competing with the form. As the user progresses through the steps
the background visualization gradually becomes more complete — more nodes appear, more
connections form — visually suggesting that by completing the registration they are
bringing their farm to life in the system.

Step one fields animate in with a staggered reveal — each field appears 60 milliseconds
after the previous one, sliding up from eight pixels below its final position and fading
in simultaneously. This entrance animation makes the form feel curated and intentional.

The password strength meter is not a horizontal bar. It is a circular arc that fills
clockwise as the password becomes stronger. In the center of the arc is a single word
— Weak, Fair, Good, or Strong — in the appropriate color. The arc goes from red at
Weak through amber to saffron-gold at Good and full forest-green at Strong. The
animation of the arc filling is fluid and continuous as the user types.

The farm GPS auto-detect button on step two, when clicked, triggers a brief animation
sequence. A small ripple radiates outward from the button, then the latitude and
longitude fields fill simultaneously with a counting animation — the numbers roll up
to their final values like an odometer. A small animated pin drops onto a miniature
map preview that appears below the coordinates field showing the farm location.

The plan selection cards on step three are not simple boxes. They are large interactive
cards that respond to hover with a three-dimensional perspective tilt — the card rotates
up to three degrees on the horizontal axis and two degrees on the vertical axis in the
direction of the cursor. The selected plan card has a saffron-gold border that traces
around the perimeter of the card in a single continuous stroke animation when selected.

The final Create My Farm Account button has an unusual treatment. After the user checks
the Terms checkbox the button seems to wake up — it brightens slightly and a subtle
pulsing glow appears around it, as if it was waiting. When clicked it fills with a
counter-clockwise sweeping animation in a darker shade, then transitions to a loading
state where the button text is replaced by a sequence showing three steps completing
in real time — Creating your account, Setting up your farm, Almost ready — before
the success animation.

Output a complete Registration Page ultra design specification covering the vertical
timeline navigation, the slide transition between steps, the animated background
visualization, the staggered field entrance animations, the circular password strength
meter, the GPS auto-detect sequence, the 3D plan selection cards, and the final
button activation choreography.

## 4. MAIN DASHBOARD ULTRA DESIGN PROMPTS

---

### Prompt 4.1 — The Farm Intelligence Command Center

You are the lead experience designer describing the Main Dashboard as the central
nervous system of the entire platform — a space where the farmer arrives every morning
and gets the complete intelligence briefing on his farm in under ten seconds.

The dashboard uses a sophisticated multi-panel layout that would not look out of place
in a mission control room or a high-end trading terminal, but with the warmth and
clarity of a beautifully designed magazine spread.

The top section of the dashboard is the Daily Briefing Band — a full-width horizontal
strip that spans the entire content area below the navigation. This band contains five
key metrics displayed as elegant stat cards arranged horizontally with equal spacing.
The five metrics are Today's Milk Yield in litres shown against yesterday and the seven-day
average, Today's Active Alerts count in red if above zero and in forest-green if zero,
Today's Estimated Revenue in rupees, Herd Health Index as a number from zero to one
hundred, and Labour Attendance as a fraction of total workers confirmed present. Each
stat card uses the display typeface for the primary number at a large size and the body
typeface for the label and comparison text at a small size. The cards have no external
border between them — they are separated only by a one-pixel hairline divider. This
creates a single unified band of intelligence.

Below the Daily Briefing Band the dashboard splits into three columns. The left column
is the narrowest at twenty five percent of the content width and contains the Activity
Feed — a chronological stream of events on the farm such as Cow 47 milked, yield 18L,
above average, Worker Suresh marked present, Alert resolved — Mastitis risk on Cow 12
cleared. Each event has a small icon, a timestamp in a relative format like 14 min ago,
and a colored left border indicating event category. The feed scrolls vertically and
new events appear at the top with a brief fade-in animation.

The center column is the widest at fifty percent and contains the primary visualization
area. This area displays a large area chart showing the last thirty days of milk
production as the hero visualization. The chart uses the smooth gradient area chart
style from the design system — saffron-gold line with a fade to transparent fill. Above
the chart are three toggle buttons to switch between viewing Milk Production, Revenue,
and Labour Cost on the same chart. The active toggle is highlighted in saffron-gold.
Below the main chart is a secondary chart showing the current month's daily breakdown
as fine vertical bars. The color of each bar encodes quality — greener bars represent
higher quality milk days and more amber bars represent lower quality days.

The right column at twenty five percent width contains stacked intelligence widgets.
The first widget is the Herd Health Heatmap — a compact grid where each cell represents
one animal identified by its ID number. The cell color encodes the animal's current
health status from deep green for healthy to warm red for attention needed. Hovering
over any cell shows a tooltip with the animal's name, health score, last alert, and
a micro-sparkline of their health trend. The second widget is the Top Alerts panel
showing the three most critical current alerts as compact cards with the alert type,
the affected animal, the time since alert was raised, and a single Resolve button. The
third widget is the Weather and Feeding Intelligence card showing today's temperature
and humidity with a note on whether feed requirements need adjustment.

The navigation sidebar uses the ultra design specification from the component library.
The farm selector at the top of the sidebar is a unique component — it shows the current
farm name with a small aerial photo of the farm as the background of the selector card.
Clicking it reveals a dropdown for farms that a multi-farm user manages.

Every number on the dashboard that updates with live data uses a subtle counter animation
when its value changes — the number rolls from the old value to the new value in three
hundred milliseconds. This makes the dashboard feel genuinely live.

The empty state for a new farm that has not yet connected any sensors or entered data
is itself a beautiful design moment. Instead of a generic illustration it shows the same
abstract farm network background from the registration page, now fully illuminated and
connected, with a centered message saying Your farm is connected. Let us add your first
data and a single saffron-gold button.

Output a complete Main Dashboard ultra design specification covering the Daily Briefing
Band, the three-column layout, the hero chart with toggle system, the Herd Health
Heatmap, the Activity Feed, the Alerts panel, the live number animations, and the
new farm empty state design.

## 5. HEALTH MONITORING MODULE ULTRA DESIGN PROMPTS

---

### Prompt 5.1 — The Herd Intelligence Theater

You are a visualization designer describing the Health Monitoring Module as a spatial
theater of cattle data — a place where the farmer can see, feel, and understand the
health of every animal in his herd simultaneously.

The Health Monitoring Module's hero feature is the Barn View. This is not a table or
a list. It is a top-down schematic visualization of the farm's cattle housing area.
The barn layout is represented as a clean architectural floor plan drawing in thin
lines — stalls are shown as rectangular cells arranged in rows as they physically exist
in the farm. Each stall cell contains an animal if occupied. The animal is represented
as a small rounded rectangle within the stall. The color of this rectangle encodes the
animal's real-time health status using a warm-to-cool spectrum. Deep forest green means
excellent health with all sensors in normal range. A warm amber means the system has
detected subtle anomalies worth monitoring. Warm red means an active alert requires
attention. The barn view is live — it updates as sensor data arrives. When a new alert
triggers the affected animal's rectangle pulses three times before settling into its
alert color.

Above the Barn View is a filter and focus row. It contains toggle pills to filter the
Barn View by showing Only Healthy, Only Monitoring, Only Alert, or All animals. A search
input allows the farmer to type a cow name or ID and immediately highlight that animal
in the barn view with a spotlight effect while dimming all others. A Group selector
allows viewing only cattle from a specific group such as lactating, dry, or calves.

Clicking on any animal in the Barn View triggers an animated transition. The clicked
animal's cell expands and the rest of the barn view zooms out and fades slightly. An
Animal Detail Panel slides in from the right side covering the right third of the screen.
This panel shows the animal's photograph at the top, their name and ID in the display
typeface, their breed and age, and then a set of real-time sensor gauges.

The sensor gauges are circular gauge components designed to look like precision
instruments. Each gauge has a thin circular track, a colored indicator arc that fills
to show the current value relative to the healthy range, a needle that points to the
current value, and the numeric value in the center in the display typeface. The healthy
range is shown as a green arc on the track and the current value's arc changes color
as it moves toward the edges of the healthy range. Six gauges are shown for Activity
Level, Rumen Temperature, Milk Yield Today, Somatic Cell Count, Body Condition Score,
and Rumination Time.

Below the gauges is a timeline chart showing the last seventy two hours of sensor data
for the selected animal. The chart allows zooming in to any time period by clicking and
dragging across the time axis. When zoomed in the chart shows more granular data points
and the chart line becomes more detailed.

Below the timeline is the Health History section showing a chronological list of every
health event, alert, vet visit, and treatment for this animal. Each event type has a
distinct icon and color. The list is paginated with a Show More button.

Output a complete Health Monitoring Module ultra design specification covering the Barn
View spatial representation, the filter and spotlight system, the animal selection
transition, the circular sensor gauges, the zoomable timeline chart, and the health
history event list.

## 6. PROFIT ANALYSIS MODULE ULTRA DESIGN PROMPTS

### Prompt 6.1 — The Farm Financial Intelligence Dashboard

You are a financial data visualization specialist designing the Profit Analysis Module
as the most beautiful and informative financial dashboard ever built for an Indian
dairy farmer.

The Profit Analysis Module opens with a full-width hero section called the Financial
Pulse. This section contains a large primary number showing the current month's net
profit or loss in the display typeface at an extremely large size — sixty-four pixels
or larger. The color of this number is forest-green for profit and warm red for loss.
Beside the number in smaller text shows the change from last month as both a rupee
amount and a percentage. Below this hero number is a horizontal sparkline showing the
last twelve months of profit and loss as a single flowing line above and below a zero
axis. Profitable months show the line above the axis in green fill and loss months
show it below the axis in red fill. This single line chart tells the annual story at
a glance.

Below the hero section is the Waterfall Analysis — the most important chart on the
page for a farmer's understanding of his finances. This chart shows a horizontal
waterfall where the leftmost bar is Total Revenue, then deductions are shown as red
bars flowing downward — Feed Costs, Labour Costs, Veterinary Costs, Energy and
Utilities, Loan Repayments, and Other Costs. Each deduction bar is sized proportionally
to its actual rupee value. The final bar on the right shows Net Profit or Loss in the
appropriate color. Above each bar is its rupee value in a small label. Below each bar
is the percentage of revenue it represents. Hovering over any bar shows a detailed
breakdown tooltip. Clicking any bar navigates to that cost category's detail page.

The Labour Costs bar in the waterfall is a special treatment — it is divided into two
segments stacked vertically within the same bar. The lower segment in a solid warm
amber shows Cash Labour Costs and the upper segment in a hatched pattern of the same
amber shows Non-Cash Labour Cost Value. This visual distinction immediately communicates
to the farmer that his labour cost is larger than his cash payment alone.

The right side of the page shows three KPI cards arranged vertically. The first card
shows Milk Revenue Per Litre in large type with a trend arrow. The second shows Total
Cost Per Litre with a breakdown of how many paise from every rupee of milk price goes
to feed, labour, vet, and other costs shown as a micro-bar chart inside the card. The
third shows Profit Per Animal Per Day — the single number that tells a farmer how
much each cow is earning or costing per day after all expenses.

Below the waterfall and KPI section is the Monthly Trend Comparison — a side-by-side
bar chart comparing this year's monthly revenue, costs, and profit against last year's
equivalent months. The bars are grouped in threes for each month. Revenue bars are in
saffron-gold, cost bars are in warm amber, and profit bars are in forest-green. A thin
dotted line connects the profit bars between years showing the trend of improvement.

Output a complete Profit Analysis Module ultra design specification covering the
Financial Pulse hero section, the Waterfall Analysis chart with the segmented Labour
bar, the three KPI cards, the micro-bar chart inside the cost-per-litre card, and
the year-on-year comparison chart.

## 7. LABOUR SALARY MODULE ULTRA DESIGN PROMPTS

### Prompt 7.1 — The Labour Intelligence Panel

You are a product designer creating the Labour Salary Module as a place where the
farmer has complete and instant clarity on his workforce's cost, contribution, and status.

The Labour Module home screen uses a worker-centered card grid layout. Each worker is
represented by a richly detailed Worker Card that is significantly more informative than
a typical list row.

Each Worker Card is one hundred and sixty pixels tall and spans the full width of its
column. The left side of the card shows the worker's photograph in a square with rounded
corners. If no photo exists the space shows the worker's initial in the display typeface
on a background color derived from the worker's name using a deterministic color
algorithm — the color is always the same for the same name and is always from the
platform's warm color palette. To the right of the photograph is a two-column layout
within the card. The left column shows the worker's name in the display typeface,

## 11. ANIMATION AND MOTION DESIGN PROMPTS

---

### Prompt 11.1 — The Complete Motion Language

You are a motion design director defining the complete animation and transition
system for the platform. Every motion must have a purpose. Nothing moves for decoration.
Every animation communicates meaning, guides attention, or confirms action.

Define the core timing and easing vocabulary for the platform. All animations use
one of five durations — Ultra Fast at one hundred milliseconds for micro-feedback
like button press states and toggle activations, Fast at two hundred milliseconds for
element state changes like hover effects and input focus, Medium at three hundred
milliseconds for component transitions like panel slides and tab switches, Slow at
five hundred milliseconds for page-level transitions and major layout changes, and
Cinematic at eight hundred milliseconds to one point two seconds for hero moments
like the post-login dashboard reveal and the application detail page opening.

The easing curves follow a philosophy that things accelerate quickly and decelerate
gracefully — matching how physical objects in the real world behave. The standard ease
is a cubic bezier that spends the first twenty percent of its time accelerating and
the remaining eighty percent decelerating. The exit ease is the opposite — things
leave quickly. The spring ease is used for playful moments like card hover lifts and
it has a slight overshoot that settles back.

Page transitions use a consistent directional logic. Navigating forward into a deeper
section slides the new content in from the right. Navigating back slides the content
in from the left. Modal panels and detail sheets slide in from the bottom on mobile
and from the right on desktop. Dismissing them reverses the direction. This consistent
directionality builds a spatial mental model of the application architecture.

Chart data loading animations reveal data progressively rather than appearing
instantaneously. For area charts the line draws from left to right over eight hundred
milliseconds and the fill fades in simultaneously. For bar charts the bars grow upward
from the baseline with a slight spring overshoot. For the Barn View heatmap the cells
fill in their health status colors sequentially in a wave pattern from left to right
across the barn layout. For gauges the indicator arc sweeps from the minimum position
to the current value over six hundred milliseconds with the number counting up in sync.

Number counter animations are used whenever a displayed number changes due to live
data updates. The current number slides upward and fades out while the new number
slides up from below and fades in simultaneously. The duration is two hundred
milliseconds. This makes the dashboard feel genuinely live without being distracting.

Error and alert animations use a horizontal shake pattern — the affected element
moves left and right three times over four hundred milliseconds with decreasing
amplitude. This is reserved exclusively for validation errors and failed actions.
It communicates urgency through physical agitation without using the overused red
flash pattern.

Success animations use an upward float and fade pattern — the confirmed element
briefly moves up four pixels and increases in brightness before settling back. This
upward movement subtly communicates uplift and positive outcome.

The loading state for entire pages or major sections uses a skeleton screen animation
where placeholder shapes appear in the layout positions of the expected content, with
a shimmer animation sweeping across them from left to right. The shimmer uses a warm
gold gradient to match the platform's color language — even the loading state feels
on-brand.

Output a complete Motion Language specification covering the five timing durations,
the easing curve philosophy for each curve type, the directional page transition
system, the chart reveal animations for each chart type, the number counter animation,
the error shake animation, the success uplift animation, and the skeleton loading
screen system.

## 12. MICRO-INTERACTION AND DETAIL DESIGN PROMPTS

---

### Prompt 12.1 — The Details That Make It Extraordinary

You are a detail-obsessed UI craftsperson describing all the micro-interactions and
finishing details that separate a good interface from an unforgettable one.

The cursor behavior changes contextually across the application. On standard clickable
elements the cursor is the standard pointer. On the Barn View cattle cells the cursor
changes to a custom cursor — a small crosshair with a circular ring suggesting
targeting or selecting. On chart areas the cursor changes to a fine vertical hairline
that follows the horizontal mouse position exactly, serving as a chart scrubber. On
draggable elements the cursor shows a grab hand. These cursor changes happen
instantaneously on enter and exit.

Every input field that validates its content in real time uses a subtle right-edge
indicator that animates from gray to green or red as the validation state changes.
This indicator is a three-pixel wide colored strip that grows from zero height to the
full field height when the field first receives a valid or invalid value.

Checkboxes use a custom animation. The check mark is not simply toggled on and off.
When checked the box border transitions to the saffron-gold color over one hundred
milliseconds, then the box fills with the gold color from left to right over eighty
milliseconds, then the custom check mark path draws itself from start to end over
one hundred and fifty milliseconds. Unchecking reverses the path animation, then
the fill color sweeps out to the right, then the border returns to neutral.

Toggle switches animate like a physical switch. The thumb slides across the track
and there is a slight compression of the thumb at the start of travel and a slight
spring extension at the end, giving a tactile quality to the interaction.

The search input field has a unique behavior. When the user begins typing the search
field expands horizontally to take more horizontal space, pushing adjacent elements
gently to the sides. Results appear below in a dropdown that blurs in from above — the
first result appears closest to full opacity and subsequent results fade in at slightly
lower opacity on arrival, creating a depth-of-field effect in the results list.

Scroll-triggered reveals are used for the lower sections of long pages. As the user
scrolls content into view each new section fades in and translates up by sixteen pixels.
This is a gentle effect that does not draw attention to itself but makes the page feel
alive as it is explored.

The sidebar navigation collapse and expand animation is not a simple width change.
The labels fade out quickly while the width collapses, and the icons remain perfectly
centered throughout the transition. On expanding the icons shift left first while
the width grows, then the labels fade in only after the animation is complete. This
staged animation prevents text wrapping during the transition.

Data tables highlight an entire row when the user's cursor enters it using a warm
background transition that animates in over one hundred milliseconds. When the cursor
leaves the highlight fades out over two hundred milliseconds — slightly slower on exit
to avoid a harsh cut.

Tooltips appear after a three hundred millisecond hover delay with a scale-up animation
from ninety to one hundred percent and a simultaneous opacity fade from zero to full.
They disappear immediately without animation on cursor exit. The delay prevents tooltip
flickering when the user moves their cursor across the screen without intending to
read any tooltip.

Alert badges on navigation items use a pulse animation — the badge gently scales from
one hundred to one hundred and fifteen percent and back twice per second when there are
unread critical alerts. The animation stops once the user opens the alerts section.

The data export button uses an especially satisfying animation sequence. When clicked it
shows a brief loading animation of files stacking, then transitions to a progress state
with a percentage indicator, then transitions to a success state where the button briefly
shows a downward-pointing arrow before returning to its default state. The entire
sequence takes no more than three seconds for small exports.

Output a complete Micro-Interaction and Detail specification covering the cursor behavior
system, the input field validation indicators, the checkbox and toggle animations, the
search field expansion behavior, the scroll-triggered reveal system, the sidebar
collapse animation, the table row hover behavior, the tooltip delay and animation,
the alert badge pulse, and the export button sequence.

## WHAT MUST NEVER APPEAR IN THIS INTERFACE

The following design choices are strictly forbidden and must be avoided in every
screen, every component, and every prompt implementation of this platform.

No white backgrounds. The platform is dark-first without exception.

No generic blue as a primary color. The saffron-gold accent is the only primary action
color.

No Inter or Roboto as the primary typeface. The display serif and the custom grotesque
are the only typefaces used.

No gradients that go from purple to blue or from pink to orange. These are generic AI
product aesthetics with no connection to this platform's identity.

No pie charts anywhere on the platform. They are banned completely as a data
visualization type.

No default chart library styling. Every chart is customized to the platform design system.

No modal dialogs for information that can be shown inline. Modals are reserved only for
actions that require the user to make a binary decision before proceeding.

No snackbar notifications at the bottom of mobile screens for critical alerts. Critical
alerts use prominent banners that demand acknowledgment.

No carousel with dots as navigation indicators. All carousels in this platform use
explicit arrow controls and a progress indicator.

No empty states with the message No data found in plain gray text without illustration
or guidance. Every empty state is an opportunity to guide the user to their next action.
