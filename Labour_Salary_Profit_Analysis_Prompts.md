## A NOTE FROM THE FIELD

Having spent fifty years working with small scale cattle farms across Tamil Nadu, Karnataka,
Maharashtra, Uttar Pradesh, and Andhra Pradesh, I have seen firsthand how labour costs
silently eat into a farmer's profits without him even realising it. A farmer in Erode
managing twenty five cattle pays his two permanent workers a fixed monthly wage, gives
daily wages to seasonal helpers during calving season, feeds the workers two meals a day,
gives festival bonuses during Pongal and Diwali, and still does not know at the end of
the month whether he made a profit or a loss. He just knows milk was sold and money came
in. This module exists to change that completely.

Labour is the second biggest cost on any small cattle farm in India after feed. It is also
the most misunderstood cost because it is personal. The worker is often a family member,
a longtime trusted hand, or a person from the same village. So farmers never track it
properly. This prompt guide will help build an AI system that tracks every rupee of labour
cost, connects it directly to farm output, and shows the farmer exactly how much each
worker contributes to or reduces his profit every single month.

### Prompt 1.1 — Real World Labour Categories on Indian Small Cattle Farms

You are an agricultural expert with fifty years of direct experience working with small
scale cattle farmers across every major dairy state in India including Tamil Nadu,
Karnataka, Maharashtra, Gujarat, Uttar Pradesh, Punjab, Rajasthan, and Andhra Pradesh.

Describe in complete detail the real world categories of labour found on Indian small
scale cattle farms with between ten and two hundred cattle. Do not describe corporate
dairy farms or cooperative managed farms. Describe only the family-run and small
owner-operated farms that make up more than ninety percent of Indian dairy production.

The first category is permanent live-in labour. These are workers who stay on the farm
premises, are given a room or shed to sleep in, receive two or three meals per day from
the farmer's household, and are paid a fixed monthly salary. They work every single day
including Sundays and festivals unless the farmer gives them a day off. They handle all
daily tasks including the morning and evening milking, feeding the cattle, cleaning the
shed, washing the animals, and basic health monitoring. In most farms in Tamil Nadu and
Karnataka this person is called the Pasuvadi or the Maadukara. In Maharashtra he is
called the Gavaladi. Describe how their salary is structured, what additional benefits
they receive beyond cash, how disputes about pay are usually handled, and what typically
causes them to leave a farm.

The second category is permanent non-live-in labour. These workers come to the farm
twice a day, once in the early morning for the first milking session and once in the
evening for the second milking session. They live in their own homes in the nearby
village or town. They receive a fixed monthly salary but no food or accommodation. In
some farms they are paid per milking session rather than per month. Describe the typical
agreement structure, the common understanding about what happens when a worker is sick
or absent, and how farmers in different states handle this differently.

The third category is casual daily wage labour. These workers are hired on an as-needed
basis, typically during high-demand periods such as calving season when multiple cows
deliver within the same week, during illness of a permanent worker, during the construction
or cleaning of a new shed, or during the harvest of green fodder on the farm's own land.
They are paid a daily rate that varies significantly by state and by season. Describe the
typical daily rate ranges in different states, how payment is made, whether they receive
food on working days, and how the farmer decides when to hire them.

The fourth category is family labour. In most small farms the farmer himself works full
time and one or two family members including spouse, children, or parents contribute
significant labour without receiving any formal salary. This hidden labour is the most
undervalued and undertracked cost in Indian dairy farming. Describe why tracking family
labour is important for true profit calculation, how to assign a value to it, and the
cultural sensitivity required when presenting this information to the farmer.

The fifth category is contract labour for specific tasks. Some farms hire contractors for
tasks such as artificial insemination which is performed by a trained technician on a
per-visit fee basis, veterinary visits on a per-call basis, fodder cutting using a rented
machine with an operator, and milk transport to the cooperative using a hired vehicle and
driver. Describe how these contract costs differ from employment costs and how they
should be tracked separately.

Output a complete real-world description of all five labour categories found on Indian
small scale cattle farms including the cultural context, the informal agreements that
govern these relationships, the regional variations in practice, and the challenges each
category poses for accurate cost tracking.

### Prompt 1.2 — Why Labour Cost Tracking Fails on Indian Farms

You are a rural development economist who has spent fifty years studying the financial
management practices of small dairy farmers across India.

Describe in plain and direct language every reason why labour cost tracking fails on
Indian small scale cattle farms today, and explain the real financial consequences of
this failure that most farmers do not realise.

The first reason is that farmers mix personal and farm expenses without any separation.
The permanent live-in worker's salary is paid from the same money used to buy groceries
for the family. The food given to the worker is cooked in the same kitchen that feeds
the farmer's children. There is no mental accounting separation between what is a farm
expense and what is a household expense. Explain how this mixing hides the true labour
cost and why a farmer who thinks he is making a profit of five thousand rupees per month
may actually be losing money when all labour is properly accounted for.

The second reason is that advances and loans to workers are not tracked as business
transactions. When a worker asks for two thousand rupees in advance before his salary
date the farmer gives it from his pocket and makes a mental note to deduct it. Often the
deduction is forgotten, partially applied, or waived out of sympathy when the worker
explains a family crisis. Over twelve months these untracked advances on a two-worker
farm can total fifteen to twenty thousand rupees of pure loss that the farmer never
accounts for. Explain this problem with specific examples relevant to Tamil Nadu farmers.

The third reason is that non-cash benefits are never counted. The worker who receives
two meals per day, a place to sleep, and occasional cash gifts for his children's school
fees is receiving much more than his stated monthly salary. If his stated salary is six
thousand rupees per month but his two daily meals cost the household eight hundred rupees
per month in food expenses, his actual cost to the farm is six thousand eight hundred
rupees. Very few farmers calculate this. Explain how to calculate the true all-in cost
of each worker including every non-cash benefit.

The fourth reason is that seasonal overtime and extra work during calving, breeding
season, and festivals is simply absorbed without any additional tracking or payment
adjustment. A worker who normally manages twenty cattle suddenly manages thirty for
three weeks when the farmer buys additional animals. His workload increased by fifty
percent but his salary stayed the same. Explain both why this seems fair to the farmer
culturally and why it actually represents a hidden subsidy the worker is providing that
should be factored into productivity calculations.

The fifth reason is that farmers do not connect labour cost to milk output. A farmer
knows he pays his worker six thousand rupees per month. He does not know that his worker
is responsible for the milking of fifteen out of his twenty cattle and that those fifteen
cattle produce one hundred and twenty litres per day. So the labour cost per litre
produced by that worker is one rupee sixty paise. If the worker is absent for five days
and production drops by forty litres per day for those five days, the farmer loses two
thousand rupees of milk revenue but only saves one thousand rupees of daily wage cost
during that absence. Most farmers never calculate this. Explain this connection clearly
and explain why it is the most powerful insight the AI system can deliver.

Output a complete analysis of all five failure reasons with specific numerical examples
relevant to small farms in Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, and Maharashtra, explaining the
cumulative financial impact of not tracking labour costs properly.

### Prompt 2.2 — Family Labour Tracking and Valuation

You are a farm economics specialist with fifty years of experience helping Indian
farmers understand the true cost of their operations including the hidden value of
family labour.

Design the complete approach for tracking and valuing family labour on a small Indian
cattle farm in a way that is culturally sensitive, practically useful, and financially
accurate.

Explain the challenge clearly. When a farmer's wife wakes up at four thirty in the
morning to help with the morning milking six days a week, that is three hours of skilled
labour per day, eighteen hours per week, approximately seventy two hours per month. At
the market rate for a permanent morning milker in Tamil Nadu, that labour is worth
between two thousand and three thousand rupees per month. But the farmer never counts
it as a cost. His profit calculation assumes that labour was free. This is the single
biggest distortion in Indian farm profit calculations.

Describe how the system should present this concept to the farmer. The framing must not
feel like a criticism of his family. The message should be that knowing the true value
of family contribution allows the farmer to understand what would happen if that family
member was not available, what it would cost to replace them with hired labour, and
whether the farm is truly profitable at that cost level.

Design the family member registration process. It should be separate from the hired
worker registration and clearly labeled as Family Contribution Tracking. The farmer
should be able to add each family member who contributes labour, the tasks they perform,
the approximate hours per day, and the days per week. The system should then calculate
the imputed value of their contribution using the local daily wage rate for that type
of task.

Describe how the system should present family labour in the profit analysis. It should
appear as a separate line item called Family Labour Value in the cost section with a
note explaining that this represents the equivalent cost of hiring someone to do the
same work. It should be clearly distinguished from paid labour costs so the farmer
understands the difference between actual cash expenses and equivalent value calculations.

Describe how the system should handle the situation where the farmer's calculation of
family labour shows that the farm is profitable when cash expenses only are counted but
unprofitable when family labour is valued at market rates. This is a sensitive finding
that must be presented constructively with suggestions for how to improve productivity
or reduce costs rather than simply showing a loss.

Output a complete specification for the family labour tracking and valuation feature
including the registration flow, the calculation methodology, the display in profit
analysis, and the communication strategy for presenting uncomfortable financial truths
to the farmer.

### Prompt 3.1 — Daily Attendance Recording

You are a mobile product designer who has spent years building technology for
semi-literate farmers in rural India. You understand that the interface must work
on a basic Android smartphone with a screen that may have cracks, in bright outdoor
sunlight, with fingers that may be wet or dirty from farm work, and for a user who
may read Kannada or Tamil but not English fluently.

Design the complete daily attendance recording experience for the Labour Salary module.

The attendance recording screen must be the simplest possible interaction. When the

the local language, their role, and three large buttons labeled Present, Absent, and
Half Day. The farmer taps one button per worker and the attendance is recorded. The
entire process for five workers should take less than thirty seconds.

Describe how to handle the common Indian farm reality where the farmer does not mark
attendance every day. If attendance has not been marked for the current day by eight

salary for daily wage workers or as a standard working day for monthly salary workers

change this convention with a note that historical records will not be changed retroactively.

Describe the special attendance categories beyond Present, Absent, and Half Day that
are relevant to Indian farm labour. These include Sick Leave where the worker is absent

payment, Compensatory Off where a worker who worked extra on a previous holiday takes
a day off in lieu, and Absent Without Notice which is treated differently from announced
leave in salary calculations.

Output a complete attendance recording system specification covering the daily marking
interface, the backdating system, the half-day calculation rules, all special attendance
categories, and the notification and reminder strategy appropriate for rural Indian
farmers.

### Prompt 3.2 — Task Assignment and Completion Tracking

You are a farm operations specialist describing how to track which specific tasks each
worker completes each day and how this connects to farm output and salary justification.

Design the task tracking system for daily farm labour in a way that is practical for
small Indian cattle farms.

Define the standard task library for a dairy cattle farm. Each task has a name, the
typical time required to complete it, the skill level required, and the number of
cattle it applies to as a scaling factor. The core tasks are Morning Milking which
involves connecting milking equipment or hand milking, collecting milk, and measuring
yield per cow, Evening Milking which is the same process repeated, Morning Feeding
which involves measuring and distributing concentrate feed and green fodder to each
animal, Evening Feeding, Fresh Water Supply which involves ensuring all water troughs
are clean and full, Shed Cleaning which involves removing dung, washing the floor,
and spreading dry material, Animal Health Observation which involves checking each

list that was set during registration. Every morning when the farmer marks attendance
the system automatically creates that day's task list for each present worker based on
their defaults. The farmer can then modify the assignments for that day if needed, for
example if one worker is doing a special task.

task list for each worker and marks tasks as Completed, Partially Completed, or Not Done.
For critical tasks like milking the system should also capture a simple yes or no
question asking whether the worker followed the correct milking process. This quality
observation feeds into the monthly worker performance summary.

Describe how task completion connects to salary. For permanent monthly salary workers
task completion history is used only for performance review and is not directly deducted
from salary. For daily wage workers absent without notice or workers who consistently
leave tasks incomplete the system should flag this for the farmer's attention and suggest
whether to continue with that worker for the next engagement.

Output a complete task tracking specification covering the standard task library, the
task assignment process, the completion recording interface, the quality observation
system, and the connection between task completion and salary calculations.

### Prompt 4.1 — Salary Calculation Engine for Indian Farm Labour

You are a farm payroll specialist with fifty years of experience managing labour
payments on Indian small dairy farms across multiple states. You understand every
variation in how Indian farmers pay their workers.

Design the complete salary calculation engine for the Labour module, covering every
payment structure used on Indian cattle farms.

The first structure is monthly fixed salary. The worker receives the same amount every
month regardless of the number of days worked, with deductions only for days absent
beyond an agreed free leave allowance. Most permanent live-in workers in South India
are paid this way. Describe how to calculate the per-day rate from the monthly salary
for deduction purposes using the actual days in the month rather than a fixed thirty.
Describe how to handle months with public holidays and whether those days are paid or
unpaid based on the farmer's setup choice. Describe the standard practice of giving
one day of paid leave per month that is common in Tamil Nadu and Karnataka.

The second structure is daily wage. The worker is paid a fixed amount per day worked
with no payment on days not worked. Common for casual and seasonal workers across
India. Describe how the system tracks the days worked in a month, applies the daily
rate, and calculates the total due. Describe how to handle the common practice of
paying daily wage workers on Saturdays for the preceding week rather than waiting
until month end.

The third structure is per session payment. The worker is paid for each milking session
they attend. A full day equals two sessions. A worker who attends only the morning
session receives half the daily rate. This structure is common in peri-urban farms
near cities in Maharashtra and Gujarat where workers have other jobs. Describe how
to track sessions attended and calculate monthly payment.

The fourth structure is the piece-rate or output-linked payment. Some farms in Punjab
and Haryana pay workers a small bonus per litre of milk collected beyond a daily
target. For example a worker who milks fifteen cows gets a base wage plus fifty paise
per litre above three hundred litres per day. Describe how to configure the base wage,
the target quantity, and the per-unit bonus, and how to calculate the monthly total
using actual milk yield data already present in the platform.

Describe how the system handles the last month calculation when a worker joins or
leaves mid-month. The calculation must be pro-rated based on actual days of employment
in that month. Describe how to generate the final settlement amount for a departing
worker that includes unpaid salary, any outstanding bonus owed, and deductions for
any advances that were not recovered before departure.

Describe how the salary calculation summary is presented to the farmer before payment
is confirmed. Show a breakdown that includes the base salary or wages earned, any
attendance deductions, any advance deductions, any bonus additions, the value of
meals provided if the farmer wants to track this, and the final net amount to pay.
The farmer should be able to review this summary and make manual adjustments with
a note before confirming payment.

Output a complete salary calculation engine specification covering all four payment
structures, the pro-rating logic for partial months, the final settlement calculation,
and the payment confirmation review interface.

### Prompt 4.2 — Non-Cash Benefits Valuation

You are a rural economist specialising in the true cost of farm labour in India. You
understand that the formal salary paid in cash is only part of what a farm worker
actually receives and that ignoring non-cash benefits leads to serious underestimation
of labour costs.

Design the complete non-cash benefits tracking and valuation system for the Labour
module, built around the real practices of Indian cattle farms.

Meals are the most universal non-cash benefit on Indian small farms. A permanent
live-in worker typically receives two or three meals per day cooked by the farmer's
household. The cost of these meals varies by state, by the number of people the
worker feeds from those meals such as if the worker has a family member living with
him, and by the household's food costs. Design a meals benefit tracker where the
farmer can set a monthly meals value per worker using either a system-suggested default
based on the state average or a custom amount entered by the farmer. The system should
clearly show this as a monthly cost per worker in the labour cost analysis.

Accommodation is provided on farms where workers live in a room, a small quarters
attached to the cattle shed, or a separate worker's room on the property. Design an
accommodation benefit tracker where the farmer can assign each live-in worker to a
room and assign a notional monthly rent value to that room. Explain why assigning
a value even if the farmer built the room himself is important for understanding the
true cost of the live-in labour model versus paying a higher salary to a non-live-in
worker who finds their own housing.

Festival bonuses are given on occasions like Pongal, Diwali, Eid, Christmas, and the
farm's local deity festival. These are not legally mandated in most states for
agricultural workers but are a strong cultural obligation. A farmer who does not give
a festival bonus risks losing his best workers to other farms. Design a festival bonus
tracker that allows the farmer to record each bonus event, the amount given per worker,
and the occasion. These should be included in the annual labour cost calculation.

Annual clothing allowance or uniform is given by some farmers especially in South India
where a set of work clothes given at Pongal or harvest time is a strong tradition. Allow
the farmer to record clothing gifts per worker per year with the amount spent.

Medical support is provided informally by many farmers who pay for their worker's
doctor visits, medicines, or hospital stays. Allow the farmer to record these one-time
payments linked to a specific worker and marked as a benefit expense rather than a
loan, since these are rarely expected to be repaid.

Children's education support is given by some progressive farmers who pay school fees
or buy books for their workers' children. Allow this to be recorded per child per year
per worker.

Design how all non-cash benefits are summarised in the worker's true cost calculation.
Show two columns for each worker — Formal Cash Salary and Total True Employment Cost
which includes the cash salary plus all non-cash benefit values. Show the difference
clearly so the farmer understands the full picture of what each worker actually costs
the farm.

Output a complete non-cash benefits tracking specification covering every benefit type,
the valuation methodology, the data entry interface, and the presentation of true
employment cost versus formal salary.

### Prompt 5.1 — Advance and Loan Management

You are a rural finance specialist who has spent fifty years watching Indian farm
workers and farmers navigate the advance and loan system that is deeply embedded in
Indian agricultural labour culture.

Design the complete advance and loan management system for the Labour module, built
around the real dynamics of this practice on Indian small cattle farms.

Describe the cultural context first. On an Indian cattle farm the practice of giving
salary advances is not just a financial transaction. It is a social bond. When a
worker asks for an advance it is usually because of a genuine family emergency such
as a medical crisis, a wedding, a death in the family, or a school fee deadline. The
farmer who refuses loses the worker's loyalty and often the worker himself. The farmer
who gives advances liberally builds a loyal workforce but often loses track of the
money. The system must support the practice while making the tracking automatic and
transparent.

Design the advance recording workflow. When a farmer gives an advance to a worker
the system should make it extremely easy to record this immediately. The home screen
should have a prominent Advance Given button on the worker's card. Tapping it opens
a simple form with three fields — the amount given, the date, and a short note about
the reason. The reason field should have quick-select options like Medical, Wedding,
Festival, School Fees, Home Repair, and Other, so the farmer can record it in two
taps. The advance is immediately added to that worker's balance ledger.

Design the recovery scheduling system. When an advance is recorded the system should
ask the farmer how he wants to recover it. The options should include Deduct full
amount next salary, Deduct in equal parts over two months, Deduct in equal parts over
three months, Deduct a custom amount each month, and Do not deduct. The system should
then automatically apply the agreed deduction during each month's salary calculation
and track the remaining balance. If the farmer chooses not to deduct the system should
periodically remind him of the outstanding advance balance.

Describe the advance ledger for each worker. Every worker's profile should show a
running advance ledger with a chronological list of every advance given, the date given,
the amount, the recovery amounts deducted each month, and the current outstanding
balance. This ledger should be visible to the farmer at all times so he always knows
exactly how much each worker owes. If the outstanding advance balance for a worker
exceeds three months of their salary the system should show a warning flag.

Describe how the system handles the departure of a worker with an outstanding advance
balance. The final settlement calculation should automatically include the outstanding
advance balance as a deduction from the final salary. If the final salary is less than
the outstanding advance the system should inform the farmer of the shortfall and allow
him to record whether he recovered the balance, waived it, or it was unpaid. This
tracking is important for understanding actual farm losses.

Output a complete advance and loan management specification covering the cultural context,
the recording workflow, the recovery scheduling system, the running ledger, the warning
system, and the final settlement handling.

### Prompt 5.2 — Deductions and Adjustments

You are a payroll specialist describing all the legitimate deduction types that can
reduce a worker's monthly salary payment on an Indian small cattle farm and how each
should be calculated and recorded.

Design the complete deductions and adjustments system for the Labour module.

Absence deductions are the most common. For a monthly salary worker the daily rate is
calculated from the monthly salary divided by the number of working days in that month.
For each day marked as absent without authorisation the corresponding daily rate amount
is deducted. Describe how the system should handle the difference between authorised
leave which is not deducted and unauthorised absence which is deducted, and how the
farmer sets the number of paid leave days allowed per month per worker during initial
setup.

Late arrival and early departure deductions are applied on some farms where the farmer
tracks time more closely. Describe a simple half-day deduction system for workers who
arrive more than two hours late or leave more than two hours early without permission.
This should be optional since many small farmers do not track time closely.

Equipment or property damage deductions are sometimes applied when a worker's carelessness
causes direct financial loss to the farm, such as breaking a milking cup or damaging a
feed container. Describe how the farmer records a damage event linked to a specific
worker, the agreed deduction amount, and how this appears in the salary calculation
with a note explaining the reason.

Advance recovery deductions are the most important recurring deduction type and are
described in detail in the advance management section. In the deductions section describe
only how they appear in the monthly salary calculation sheet and how to clearly separate
them from punitive deductions so the worker can see the difference.

Accommodation and meals deductions apply in the rare case where a farmer charges workers
for their accommodation or meals. In this case the system should allow the deduction to
be configured as a fixed monthly amount per worker and automatically subtracted from the
gross salary.

Cooperative society deduction is relevant for farms in states where farm workers are
enrolled in labour welfare cooperative societies. A fixed monthly amount is deducted from
salary and sent to the society. Allow the farmer to configure this per worker if applicable.

Describe how all deductions are shown in the monthly salary slip. The slip should show
the gross amount earned at the top, then each deduction listed individually with its
amount and a short reason, then the total deductions subtracted, and finally the net
payment amount clearly shown at the bottom. The net payment amount should be shown in
large text because that is the number the farmer needs to see when counting out cash.

Output a complete deductions and adjustments specification covering all deduction types,
the calculation methodology for each, the display on the salary slip, and the farmer
interface for managing each type of deduction.

### Prompt 6.1 — Festival Bonus and Annual Payment Planning

You are a rural HR specialist with deep knowledge of the bonus and festival payment
culture on Indian cattle farms across all major dairy states.

Design the complete festival bonus and annual payment planning system for the Labour
module, reflecting the real cultural expectations and financial planning needs of
small Indian cattle farmers.

Describe the significance of festival bonuses on Indian farms. A worker who does not
receive a bonus at Pongal in Tamil Nadu or at Diwali in North India considers it a
serious disrespect and will actively look for another farm. The bonus is not just money.
It is an acknowledgement of the relationship between the farmer and the worker and of
the worker's contribution to the farm's success. A farmer who pays bonuses promptly and
generously retains good workers for decades. A farmer who pays late or pays less than
expected loses workers at the worst times of the agricultural calendar.

List all major festival bonus occasions relevant across Indian dairy farming states and
explain the typical bonus amount as a multiple or percentage of monthly salary for each.
Pongal in Tamil Nadu and Karnataka in January typically involves one full month salary

also give a Deepavali bonus of half month salary in October.

Karnataka farms particularly in the Hassan, Tumkur, Mandya, and Shivamogga districts
follow similar structures to Tamil Nadu but wages are slightly lower in rural interior

Maharashtra farms particularly in the Kolhapur, Pune, Nasik, and Ahmednagar belts have

Punjab and Haryana farms have the highest farm labour wages in India due to competition
